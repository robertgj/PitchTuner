Polynomial Interpolation

This section describes the algorithm for polynomial interpolation of the pitch estimate at the first cumulative distance function minimum.

Golub and van Loan (in Matrix Computations, 3rd edition, 1996), define the Vandermonde matrix, \(V\), as:

\[ \begin{align} V=V(x_{0},\ldots,x_{n})=\left[\begin{array}{cccc} 1 & 1 & \ldots & 1\\ x_{0} & x_{1} & \ldots & x_{n}\\ \vdots & \vdots & & \vdots\\ x_{0}^{n} & x_{1}^{n} & \ldots & x_{n}^{n} \end{array}\right] \end{align} \]

Solution of \(V^{T}a=f\) is equivalent to polynomial interpolation:

\[ \begin{equation} p(x)=\sum_{j=0}^{n}a_{j}x^{j} \end{equation} \]

where \(p(x_{i})=f_{i}\) for \(i=0\ldots n\).

For distinct \(x_{i}\) there is a unique polynomial of degree n that interpolates \((x_{0},f_{0}),\ldots\), \((x_{n},f_{n})\).

Golub and van Loan (Section 4.6.1) show an algorithm for polynomial interpolation. Given \(x(0\ldots n)\in\mathbb{R}^{n+1}\) with distinct entries and \(f=f(0\ldots n)\in\mathbb{R}^{n+1}\), the following pseudo-code overwrites \(f\) with the solution \(a=a(0\ldots n)\) to the Vandermonde system \(V(x_{0},\ldots,x_{n})^{T}a=f\):

for k=0:b-1
for i=n:-1:k+1
f(i)=(f(i)-f(i-1))/(x(i)-x(i-k-1))
end
end
for k-n-1:-1:0
for i=k:n-1
f(i)=f(i)-f(i+1)x(k)
end
end

This algorithm requires \(5n^{2}/2\) flops.

Newton's divided differences interpolation polynomial converts the Vandermonde matrix to lower triangular form. The divided differences can be written in the form of a table:

\[ \begin{align} \begin{array}{cccc} x_{0} & y_{0}\\ & & \left[y_{1},y_{0}\right]=\frac{y_{1}-y_{o}}{x_{1}-x_{0}}\\ x_{1} & y_{1} & & \left[y_{2},y_{1},y_{0}\right]=\frac{\frac{y_{2}-y_{1}}{x_{2}-x_{1}} -\frac{y_{1}-y_{0}}{x_{1}-x_{0}}}{x_{2}-x_{0}}\\ & & \left[y_{2},y_{1}\right]=\frac{y_{2}-y_{1}}{x_{2}-x_{1}} & \vdots\\ x_{2} & y_{2} & \vdots\\ \vdots & \vdots \end{array} \end{align} \]

The interpolating polynomial uses the topmost entry in each column as coefficients. More precisely, for a set of \(n+1\) data points \((x_{0},f_{0}),\ldots\), \((x_{n},f_{n})\) where no two \(x_{i}\) are the same , the interpolation polynomial in the Newton form is a linear combination of Newton basis polynomials:

\[ \begin{equation} N(x)=\sum_{j=0}^{n}c_{j}n_{j}(x) \end{equation} \]

with the Newton basis polynomials defined as:

\[ \begin{equation} n_{j}(x)=\prod_{i=0}^{j-1}(x-x_{i}) \end{equation} \]

and the coefficients defined as:

\[ \begin{equation} c_{j}=\left[y_{0},\ldots,y_{j}\right] \end{equation} \]

where \(\left[y_{0},\ldots,y_{j}\right]\) is the notation for divided differences. Thus the Newton polynomial can be written as:

\[ \begin{equation} N(x)=\left[y_{0}\right]+\left[y_{0},y_{1}\right](x-x_{0}) +\ldots+\left[y_{0},\ldots,y_{k}\right](x-x_{0}) \ldots(x-x_{n}) \end{equation} \]

Using the basis polynomials \(n_{j}(x)\) we have to solve:

\[ \begin{align} \left[\begin{array}{ccccc} 1 & & \ldots & & 0\\ 1 & x_{1}-x_{0} & & &\\ 1 & x_{2}-x_{0} & (x_{2}-x_{0})(x_{2}-x_{1}) & & \\ \vdots & \vdots & & \ddots & \vdots\\ 1 & x_{k}-x_{0} & \ldots & & \prod_{j=0}^{n}(x_{n}-x_{j}) \end{array}\right]\left[\begin{array}{c} c_{0}\\ \vdots\\ c_{n} \end{array}\right]=\left[\begin{array}{c} y_{0}\\ \vdots\\ y_{n} \end{array}\right] \end{align} \]

This matrix can be solved recursively by

\[ \begin{equation} \sum_{i=0}^{j}c_{i}n_{i}(x_{j})=y_{j},\,j=0,\ldots,n \end{equation} \]

For a quadratic:

\[ \begin{align*} c_{0}&=y_{0}\\ c_{0}+c_{1}(x_{1}-x_{0})&=y_{1}\\ c_{0}+c_{1}(x_{2}-x_{0})+c_{2}(x_{2}-x_{0}) (x_{2}-x_{1})&=y_{2} \end{align*} \]

so:

\[ \begin{align*} c_{0}&=y_{0}\\ c_{1}&=\frac{1}{x_{1}-x_{0}}\left[y_{1}-c_{0}\right]\\ c_{2}&=\frac{1}{x_{2}-x_{1}}\left[\frac{1}{x_{2}-x_{0}} (y_{2}-c_{0})-c_{1}\right] \end{align*} \]

Expanding \(p(x)\):

\[ \begin{align*} p(x) & = c_{0}+c_{1}(x-x_{0})+c_{2}(x-x_{0})(x-x_{1})\\ & = (c_{0}-x_{0}(c_{1}-c_{2}x_{1})) +(c_{1}-c_{2}(x_{1}+x_{0}))x +c_{2}x^{2} \end{align*} \]

Recall that for \(p(x)=ax^{2}+bx+c\) with \(a>0\), the minimum occurs at \((-\frac{b}{2a},c-\frac{b^{2}}{4a})\).
